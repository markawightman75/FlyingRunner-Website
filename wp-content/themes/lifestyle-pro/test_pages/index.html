The Running Charity: Interview with Alex Eagle

23 March 2015 by Sarah Wightman Leave a Comment (Edit)

The Running Charity works with homeless young people, using running as a way to engage homeless youth and help them achieve goals. The results are impressive, with over 90% of those from the programme gaining long-term accommodation, and securing employment, education or training. We caught up with Alex Eagle, director of the charity, to find out more about their innovative work and how running fits in.

What does The Running Charity do?

We work with homeless young people, aged 16-24. We provide a programme to help them get back on their feet by giving them a focus, a social group, and some specific personal goals to achieve.

Running provides the framework. There are three weekly fitness sessions, and we use running to help the young people on the programme to set and achieve goals. This gives them an opportunity to start succeeding and achieving targets, and start breaking away from the negative experiences they have often become used to.

They get a healthy and regular positive experience, a sense of inclusion and belonging, and a group of friends. They build their self-confidence and self-belief.

We also partner with existing homeless charities, healthcare services, and other organisations, so that we can channel the young people directly to the pre-existing services they need.

Quick facts:

  • In the UK 80,000 young people aged 16-24 experience homelessness each year
  • There's a clear link between homelessness and withdrawal from education, employment or training
  • Homelessness often causes mental health problems
  • Homeless young people often lack confidence, motivation and a sense of purpose
  • Homelessness not only has a major effect on the young person, but also negatively impacts wider society

How did The Running Charity get started?

I come from a background working at a Day Centre for the homeless in King’s Cross. I found that a lot of the work I did there was reactive, because funding was tight and would go straight into frontline services such as housing to deal with an immediate crisis. Although that made sense, it didn’t solve the underlying problems and didn’t really help the young people in the longer term.

I found that frustrating. I wanted to do something less reactive and with more potential for helping people in the long term.

So how did that spark the programme?

One day a guy called James came into the centre. He’d had a friend who had passed away, who was a drug user and became homeless. So James wanted to do something that both honoured his friend’s memory, but also channelled his grief into something constructive.

With huge generosity, James put in all his savings, and was clearly committed to developing a worthwhile project. As a runner, he wanted to trial a programme to get homeless young people to run marathons. It had great potential to fit well with my aim of doing something more substantial and long-term in reach, if the premise was developed and if young people were consulted about the activities that they wanted to do.

So we set about working on a programme that would use sport to do something strategic for the long term benefit of young people, not just solely for getting them to do a marathon or to get fit. It would also tap into existing services, such as the housing advice provided at the Day Centre where I worked. We wanted to shape a programme that had lasting effects.

How did you first put the idea into action?

We started the programme with 12 young people. They were all sleeping rough, finding inappropriate places like night buses to sleep, or using night shelters. They were all unemployed.

We created a programme with goal setting, where they would tick off both personal health goals, and goals that addressed their own underlying needs.

The health goals we set are usually running related, and often involve working towards running a certain distance such as a 5k. Other goals can be anything from accessing primary healthcare to getting a CV prepared. Each goal has an incentive attached, for that extra bit of motivation.

The programme gets the young people into a habit of setting a goal and achieving it.

When young people get disengaged and disenfranchised they become very used to failure. So this stops them wanting to do anything, because they feel it’s just another thing they’re going to be let down on, or where they’ll let themselves down.

So the point of the goals is to get the young people into a habit of setting a goal and achieving it. For this reason, it’s also helpful to start with simplistic goals at the beginning, so that the goals are achievable and they can begin succeeding at something as soon as possible.

A goal could be as basic as just getting to the session, as long as they’re achieving something. That can then be built on. Over time, this leads them to much bigger goals like getting a job.

How did your first intake respond to this approach?

Fundamentally, particularly with young people who are homeless, they want to get out of the situation. Their homelessness is usually circumstantial, often due to family breakdown, and they want a way out. But the longer they are stuck in a situation, the longer they deteriorate and the more it can affect their mental health.

So it’s about addressing those needs quickly. And that’s what the goals do, and the running gives them focus for this.

This first intake proved how the programme could help young people in the long term. By the end of the programme, all 12 were housed and all 12 were employed, and they still are now.

Four of them are now managers, since they did the programme in 2012.

Tell us about some of the individuals who have come through the programme.

Claude was a refugee from Rwanda, who went to school and then to university in London. When he left university, his family couldn’t give him accommodation any more, and he became homeless. The Running Charity gave him a place on the programme, supported him with his immigration application, and set him some goals including setting up work placements for him. He responded really well to the goals, and gained work experience as well as learning about personal training. He’s now training to run the London Marathon in April.

Rob (name changed) was homeless, was a heroine user, and had dropped to seven stone in weight. He came onto the programme, and through personal goals he managed to get clean from drugs, went up to a healthy 14 stone, and now helps us out with personal training.

Suzi (name changed) had become homeless as a result of a family breakdown, and had been sleeping in a night shelter. She was one of the 12 in the first intake, and she responded really well to the programme and is now a manager in a bar.

Are they still involved with the programme?

Yes. When the first intake graduated after six months, they all become mentors for the next intake, and they form our Members’ Advisory Panel. They shape the way the programme is going and act as the voice of the young people.

So we have a very grassroots approach. We make sure we take our direction from the needs of the young people first, and we are accountable to them as a priority, rather than getting distracted by having to meet the criteria of a particular funding body.

Specifically, how does the running fit in?

We do three fitness sessions a week at each hub. Those are not purely running – we do a large element of running, but we listen to what the young people want, and so we occasionally mix it up with, for example, a martial arts session or circuit training.

We do a monthly parkrun together. The 5k is an important goal that needs to be ticked off for them, to move up through their goals. We try to enter them into 10k races (some race organisers are willing to give us last minute places if they haven’t fill them all). Then we have a few entries into the marathon this year – we gained six places through the ballot.

James is a good runner, and is going to pace Claude around London this year. Claude is aiming to run it in under four hours.

Marathon training is a huge commitment. How do the young people deal with that demand?

Yes, it’s a real challenge. But through the programme we create a very positive experience through running. Lots of kids don’t enjoy sport at school and they drop out of activity as soon as they don’t have to do it at school. We made the running about having fun: we made it sociable and about becoming part of a community.

What we find is that after we’ve created that kind of positive experience, they continue with it.

Lots of our graduates meet up still and run together, independently from The Running Charity. They meet up to do parkrun together regularly, and have built long term friendships as a group.

Some of them love the running, and for others it gives them the confidence and motivation to get back into other sports that they like. In so many sports, running is the core element anyway.

Our young people take the exact same path as anyone who decides to put on their trainers for the first time and run.

Why do you think running works so well as the focus?

Running is a real leveller. Everyone can appreciate the journey you have from when you take up running.

When you first run, it’s the same whether you’re homeless or not homeless. It’s all about looking to self-improve. Our runners and our young people take the exact same path as anyone who decides to put on their trainers for the first time and run.

Running is also a very simple thing to do. You don’t need much equipment. Does that help with structuring and funding the programme?

Getting trainers is the main thing. Our main ongoing cost is for trainers, it’s the biggest expense we pay for. We’ve found some creative ways of supporting this – for example, Runner’s World get sent test trainers of new models and brands, and they donate those test trainers to us. People sometimes donate their old trainers too, which is great, and particularly helpful in getting young people started in the first place.

However, part of our incentive system is that we reward the young people with their own pair of brand new trainers. There’s nothing better than getting your own pair of new trainers and taking them out of the box and feeling like they’re your trainers! So we’ve got partners: Sweatshop give us a discount, and Mizuno have donated trainers, which really helps with this.

Where does the rest of your funding come from?

The London Marathon has given us our main grant. An organisation called Sported also give us funding, as well as Streetsmart, plus a host of smaller donations from runners and trusts.

And we’re trying to engage as many people from the running community as possible to run for us, for sponsorship in any event – 10k, mud run, marathon, anything.

We have also secured race entries into the Vitality Run series, which people can access and run for us. These include 10k and half marathons across the UK, and we are also supporting people into mud runs. People can contact me (Alex) about these places – alex.eagle@therunningcharity.org.

What are your ambitions for The Running Charity?

I would like us to achieve something wider. As well as supporting young people who are homeless, I would like us to provide a voice to advocate for those young people, and campaign to prevent them slipping through the net in the first place.

The situation for the homeless, including homeless young people, has become far worse under the current government. As well as higher levels of deprivation for the poorest in society, there have been cuts to the homeless sector and to mental healthcare and primary healthcare. There are more young people slipping through the net. For those from youth offending backgrounds, they reach a certain age and have no further support or legal entitlement. They can easily end up in a difficult situation.

We want to get to a point where we can actually give young people who have experienced homelessness a platform and a voice, and start making a real difference to policy making.

Do you think the charity will grow beyond London?

I hope so. We have already had various discussions with groups in other cities.

We currently have three locations in London. The challenge with expansion is obviously funding. The delivery is fairly inexpensive, as we have volunteers as well as paid staff, so it’s mainly equipment such as trainers.

We also want to build wider awareness and increase grassroots fundraising.

We have some high profile supporters, for example Colin Jackson is our patron, and Conrad Williams (4 x 400m European & Commonwealth gold medallist) is one of our coaches. Colin has helped us to make contacts at the BBC, so we’re hoping that Claude will be interviewed when he finishes the London Marathon.

We have also had endorsements from Seb Coe, who said The Running Charity is the embodiment of what the 2012 legacy is all about.

For runners who are reading this and would like to help, what can they do to get involved?

We’re always looking for more grassroots fundraising, so consider raising money for The Running Charity in your next event.

Also you can sponsor Claude in the marathon: uk.virginmoneygiving.com/ClaudeUmuhire

And there are opportunities to volunteer too, so you people can contact us directly to find out how to get involved.

To find out more about The Running Charity, visit: therunningcharity.org, or contact info@therunningcharity.org, or alex.eagle@therunningcharity.org
